# Editorial System

## Editorial Purpose

As Steve on TV should read like a serious institution built around a premise that happens to be funny.

The writing cannot be lazy, smug, or overcooked. The voice should create trust, specificity, and delight at the same time.

## Writing Principles

- Be precise before being clever.
- Lead with observation, not punchlines.
- Treat each entry like an artifact with context.
- Write from conviction, not from distance.
- Prefer concrete detail over generic praise.
- Let the absurdity emerge from seriousness.

## Tone Guardrails

### The Tone Should Be

- straight-faced
- observant
- slightly museum-like
- lightly amused, never desperate to prove the joke
- editorial rather than encyclopedic

### The Tone Should Not Be

- snarky
- ironic for its own sake
- meme-stuffed
- random
- parody copy that weakens the product's authority

## What Makes a Great Steve Entry

A great entry does four things:

1. Identifies the Steve clearly.
2. Explains why this Steve matters in the index.
3. Places the Steve in a broader cultural or tonal lane.
4. Leaves the reader wanting the next card in the cabinet.

The best entries feel inevitable in hindsight.

## Required Fields for Editorial Blurbs

Every published Steve entry should have:

- `summary`: one-line description of the entry
- `dek`: a sharper, more flavorful framing line
- `whyItMatters`: the editorial reason the entry earns its place
- body copy: one to three short paragraphs of grounded editorial context

The body copy should usually answer:

- what kind of Steve this is
- how the appearance works on screen
- what makes it memorable, canonical, strange, or useful

## Funny vs Serious

The ratio should be roughly:

- `80%` serious editorial framing
- `20%` dry voltage

The humor should come from exactness, ranking language, overcommitment, and cultural observation. Do not write as if the product is embarrassed by itself.

## Entry Writing Pattern

Recommended shape:

1. Define the Steve cleanly.
2. Name the specific trait, aura, or function.
3. Explain why the entry belongs in the archive.
4. Point toward a related Steve lane, collection, or contrast.

## Good Entry Writing Examples

### Example 1

`Steve Urkel is not merely a sitcom Steve. He is one of the central forces that redefined what television audiences expect a Steve to do. The suspenders, the nasal precision, the catastrophic confidence: all of it pushed the name into a new comic register.`

Why it works:

- specific
- serious
- culturally framed
- funny through conviction

### Example 2

`Steve Holt is a recurring-character masterclass in Steve compression. He enters loud, branded, and immediately legible. The performance does not require depth to produce impact; it requires exactness, and it gets there instantly.`

Why it works:

- identifies the lane
- avoids empty hype
- makes an editorial argument

## Bad Entry Writing Examples

### Example 1

`This Steve is iconic and hilarious and honestly such a vibe. We love him.`

Why it fails:

- generic praise
- no observation
- no editorial argument

### Example 2

`Here comes another Steve, because apparently TV has a lot of those guys.`

Why it fails:

- undercuts the premise
- sounds detached from the product
- treats the subject like a throwaway joke

### Example 3

`One of the greatest Steves of all time, period.`

Why it fails:

- unsupported ranking claim
- no basis for the statement

## Rules for Collections

Collections need a clear thesis. They are not buckets.

Each collection should define:

- why these entries belong together
- what angle the collection is arguing
- why the grouping improves discovery

Collection intros should sound like a curator making a case, not like a landing page description.

## Rules for Ranking Language

Use ranking language only when the basis is legible.

Good ranking language:

- "among the defining sitcom Steves"
- "a top-tier commercial Steve on aura alone"
- "one of the cleanest Steven pivots in family drama"

Bad ranking language:

- "the best ever" without criteria
- "objectively number one"
- vague superlatives with no editorial standard

If the ranking is playful, the reader should still understand the logic.

## Collections and Ranking Tone

- Be decisive.
- Be explainable.
- Do not hide behind fake neutrality.
- Do not write provocations just to manufacture heat.

## House Style Notes

- Capitalize media titles correctly.
- Use short, punchy paragraphs.
- Avoid exclamation points unless quoting source material.
- Avoid slang that will date quickly.
- Prefer concrete nouns and verbs over abstract mood words.
- Use lists sparingly in public-facing copy.

## How To Sound Like As Steve on TV

Checklist:

- Does the sentence make a specific claim?
- Does it sound like someone who actually cataloged the archive?
- Does it preserve the dignity of the premise?
- Is the funny part emerging naturally rather than being announced?
- Could this line appear in a collectible guidebook without embarrassment?

If not, tighten it until it does.
