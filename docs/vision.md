# Vision

## Product Thesis

As Steve on TV is a canonical editorial index for Steve, Stephen, Steven, and Stevie characters and on-screen people across TV, movies, and commercials.

The thesis is simple: a narrow, high-taste index can be more culturally resonant than a broad, generic database. The constraint is the product. A site that takes one funny premise seriously enough becomes useful, memorable, and shareable.

## The Wedge

The wedge is not "entertainment metadata."

The wedge is:

- one specific naming lane
- one specific media lane
- one specific tone
- one specific promise: if a Steve matters on screen, this is where you look

That level of specificity gives the product shape fast. It creates immediate identity, immediate curiosity, and a naturally opinionated editorial lens.

## Target Users

Primary users:

- pop culture obsessives who enjoy indexing, ranking, and classification
- people who like rabbit-hole discovery sites with a strong point of view
- internet users who share weirdly specific canonical references

Secondary users:

- writers and editors looking for a source of amusing but useful reference
- journalists, meme-makers, and culture account operators
- future contributors and agents helping expand the index

## Why This Idea Works

- The premise is instantly legible and inherently shareable.
- The narrowness creates memorability instead of confusion.
- The editorial voice can turn a catalog into an experience.
- The subject naturally supports search, collections, rankings, and internal linking.
- The format compounds: every new entry strengthens search surface area, collections, and related-entry rabbit holes.

## Product Pillars

### Canonical Specificity

The site wins by being narrower than anyone expects and more complete than anyone thought necessary.

### Editorial Discovery

This is not just lookup. It should guide users from one Steve to the next through relations, archetypes, collections, and surprising adjacency.

### Collectible Artifacts

Entries should feel like cards, plaques, or indexed treasures rather than plain records.

### Straight-Faced Absurdity

The product should be sincere enough that the joke never needs explaining.

## What This Is

- a curated media index
- an editorial discovery engine
- a strange-but-useful source of truth
- a framework for collections, rankings, and archetypes

## What This Is Not

- a general entertainment database
- a fandom wiki
- a social network
- a celebrity encyclopedia
- a broad naming gimmick platform for every first name
- an SEO content mill filled with thin pages

## MVP Definition

The MVP is successful when As Steve on TV launches with:

- `250+` curated entries
- strong detail pages for each Steve entry
- a browse experience that makes the catalog feel alive
- search that helps users find exact Steves and discover adjacent ones
- a starter set of editorial collections
- a clean system for adding new entries through repo-managed content

The MVP does not need full user accounts, broad community editing, recommendation engines, or a complex CMS.

## Long-Term Vision

Long term, As Steve on TV becomes the definitive Steve index on the internet and expands in depth, not looseness.

Future layers may include:

- richer editorial collections and seasonal packages
- stronger community submissions with moderation
- collectible print or digital products
- limited merch and taste-driven commerce
- sponsorships that fit the voice without becoming generic ads

The long game is a beloved niche institution, not a bloated platform.

## Monetization Direction

Monetization should emerge from taste, not interruption.

Preferred directions:

- collectible products
- limited edition editorial drops
- memberships for special collections or dispatches
- tasteful sponsorships tied to media, culture, or design
- affiliate or commerce opportunities only when they feel editorially native

Avoid default banner-ad logic. The product should feel too specific to cheapen that way.

## Key Risks

- the product drifts into a generic media database
- the writing gets too jokey and undermines credibility
- the content model becomes overbuilt before enough entries exist
- SEO incentives flatten the editorial taste
- scope expands into adjacent names, off-screen people, or unrelated media categories too early
- the UI defaults to framework boilerplate and loses the collectible feeling

## Expansion Paths

Good expansion:

- deeper Steve archetypes
- better commercial indexing
- smarter collections and thematic trails
- richer credits and provenance where editorially useful
- moderated submissions that improve coverage

Bad expansion:

- every first-name index
- every person named Steve regardless of screen relevance
- every piece of entertainment metadata
- generic recommendation feeds

## Strategic Constraints

- Stay inside Steve / Stephen / Steven / Stevie.
- Stay inside TV, movies, and commercials for MVP.
- Treat on-screen people as in scope only when the appearance itself belongs in the index.
- Keep canonical content in repo files for v1.
- Prefer static-first Next.js and build-time pages over operational complexity.
- Preserve editorial review as a core product function.
- Protect the site's identity even when growth opportunities tempt broader positioning.
