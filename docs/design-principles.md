# Design Principles

## How The Site Should Feel

As Steve on TV should feel like a collectible cabinet opened on the internet.

It should feel:

- archival but alive
- editorial but playable
- crafted rather than templated
- serious enough to be funny
- dense with discovery, never cluttered

The right reference frame is not "content platform." It is "museum gift shop catalog meets old TV guide meets obsessive personal archive."

## Visual Principles

- Use typography that feels chosen, not defaulted.
- Let cards and modules feel like artifacts, not commodity tiles.
- Build surface texture through spacing, framing, and hierarchy rather than visual noise.
- Make metadata readable without turning pages into spreadsheet slabs.
- Use color sparingly and intentionally, like a labeling system or print accent.

## UX Principles

- A user should understand the premise immediately.
- The first click should lead to the second without effort.
- Search should feel confident.
- Browse should feel like opening drawers in a cabinet.
- Collections should add thesis, not just grouping.
- Every page should offer the next meaningful rabbit hole.

## Inspiration References In Descriptive Terms

- old broadcast listings with hierarchy and density
- print ephemera from media archives
- museum labels that know when to stop talking
- fan-made internet shrines before they became productized
- editorial indexes that reward re-reading and collecting

## What To Avoid

- generic SaaS dashboards
- startup landing-page gradients with no relation to the premise
- sterile card grids with identical metadata blocks
- fan wiki clutter
- over-animated gimmicks
- default UI library vibes
- empty whitespace mistaken for taste

## Card System Philosophy

Cards are not just containers. They are the basic collectible unit.

Each card should communicate:

- the Steve
- the media context
- one reason to care
- one path to go deeper

Different card sizes should support different modes:

- compact cards for dense browse
- standard cards for search and collection grids
- featured cards for homepage and editorial framing

Cards should feel ownable and referenceable, almost like issue covers or filed index cards.

## Browse Design Behavior

- allow scanning without fatigue
- expose filters that feel editorial, not enterprise
- keep visible pathways into collections and related entries
- make density adjustable through layout, not preference panels

Browse should invite curiosity. It should never look like a table dump.

## Search Design Behavior

- prioritize exact matches clearly
- surface near matches and variant matches without confusion
- make the result list feel like found artifacts, not raw rows
- support fast refinement by media type, name variant, era, and archetype

Search should help the user feel smart, not burdened.

## Rabbit-Hole Behavior

Rabbit holes are core product behavior, not frosting.

Every entry page should lead to:

- adjacent archetypes
- same-title or same-franchise links
- name-variant contrasts
- collection appearances
- editorial "see also" trails

Related modules should feel authored, even when partially system-generated.

## Archival and Collectible Framing

- use labels, captions, and section headers that imply a catalog
- make each page feel preservable and worth sending
- preserve a sense of rarity and curation
- avoid making the archive feel exhaustive in a boring way

The user should feel they are paging through a cabinet of saved cultural objects.

## Responsive Behavior Principles

- protect hierarchy on mobile first
- keep card stacks visually distinct at small sizes
- reduce chrome before reducing information value
- keep filters and related links reachable without burying them in heavy drawers
- ensure entry pages preserve their collectible feel on handheld screens

Mobile should feel like a pocket field guide, not a collapsed desktop grid.

## Accessibility Principles

- maintain strong contrast and legible type scales
- support keyboard navigation across browse, search, and filters
- make all card actions and related-entry links screen-reader clear
- avoid relying on color alone for status or taxonomy meaning
- preserve semantic heading structure on every page

Accessibility should improve clarity, not act as a compliance afterthought.

## Delight Without Gimmick

Delight should show up through:

- precise labels
- surprising but useful relations
- satisfying card composition
- strong copy hierarchy
- moments of recognition in collections and metadata

Delight should not depend on:

- novelty animations
- random jokes
- decorative clutter
- trying too hard to prove the concept is quirky

When in doubt, choose the more durable form of charm.
